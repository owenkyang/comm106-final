A recent peer-reviewed study found that 70% of students globally now use ChatGPT as part of their academic lives (Amoah et al., 1). That is not just a lot of people, but instead a fundamental change in how students are learning and who is controlling the tools they use.

With the recent innovation in AI, its prevalence and usage seems to only be increasing with time. AI platforms like ChatGPT are constantly being used by students, industry professionals, and the general public every second. Masses of people and numerous industries are drawn to the platform for its convenience and new potential use cases. However, this convenience is beginning to create a dependency on the platform. Although the masses of people believe that the platform is helping to boost their efficiency, they are unknowingly supporting a discreet corporate narrative. More specifically, the convenience of ChatGPT is masking a transfer of power to corporations, autonomy, and privacy away from users.

A So-So Technology

To begin, ChatGPT is a type of "so-so technology" that automates tasks already done adequately by human workers. A "so-so technology" as MIT professor Daron Acemoglu and Boston University's Pascual Respreto put it is a technology that displaces employment but does not generate a boost in productivity (Brown, 1). This creates leverage for corporations to utilize these AI tools to replace human workers. However, this creates dependency on the AI platform without meaningfully improving productivity or quality of life for the majority of people. An example of where corporations sought out to replace human workers with AI was with the WGA strike where Hollywood studios attempted to utilize the AI platform to cut costs. This is a clear example of corporations utilizing this "so-so technology" to replace human workers without improving the actual work at all. Furthermore, Daron Acemoglu in his paper "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI" explains how AI will increase efficiency "no more than a 0.71% increase in total factor productivity over 10 years" (Acemoglu, 1). He also explains how the majority of AI's introductory success has been from "easy-to-learn" tasks. Acemoglu describes that AI falls short when put into situations that require context, critical decision making, and nuance. This shows how these AI platforms aren't actually improving efficiency by a large margin, but gives corporations leverage over the working class.

Corporate Imaginaries and the Loss of Autonomy

Moreover, the increasing usage of ChatGPT and similar platforms normalizes a future designed by the companies that create the platforms and feeds into socio-technical imaginaries. Socio-technical imaginaries as defined in "Future Imperfect: Science, Technology, and the Imaginations of Modernity" by Sheila Jasanoff, show "the myriad ways in which scientific and technological visions enter into the assemblages of materiality, meaning, and morality that constitute robust forms of social life" (Jasanoff, 4). This dependency on AI platforms moves the autonomy of learning and thinking away from real people and serves it on a silver platter to corporations. Corporations understand this shift of power and make it a key argument for their technology. By fabricating a sort of crisis on the idea that our current ways are slow and inefficient, corporations can establish themselves as obligatory passage points (Sadowski & Bendor, 547). Moreover, corporations that create AI platforms promote "solutionism," or the belief that all the world's issues can and should be solved with new technology (Sadowski & Bendor, 553). However, framing the friction with human effort as a "problem" devalues critical thinking and reflection that is uniquely human.

Surveillance and the Loss of Privacy

Not only does the increasing usage of ChatGPT and similar technologies normalize a future designed by their parent companies, but it also enables mass surveillance and transfers privacy away from users. Every interaction with ChatGPT is an act of data generation, and according to OpenAI's own privacy policy, that data belongs to them. Additionally, the platform logs user data ranging from device information and usage activity all the way down to the actual content of conversations. The policy also states that OpenAI can use what users type to train the models powering ChatGPT. In other words, every homework question a student asks, every problem someone at work is trying to solve, and every personal worry someone brings to the platform is training material for OpenAI. Users create the training material, but at the end of the day, the corporation owns and profits from them. Even when users try to delete their conversations, those conversations are still kept in OpenAI's servers. These are details buried in the long terms of service and privacy policy that most users never end up reading.

A Call to Think Critically

If you are a student or someone just starting out in the workforce, you have probably already used ChatGPT more times than you can count. It is something that is fast and easy to use, and your peers are probably using it too. But it is worth stopping for a second and asking whether it is actually making you better at anything, or just making things easier in the moment. Additionally, every conversation gets logged and stored, and OpenAI can use what you type to train its own models and to sell for their own profits. These companies are not offering a free and helpful tool out of the goodness of their hearts. There is a business behind it, and your data is a big part of how it runs. So next time you go to open ChatGPT, just think for a second about who else is benefiting from that conversation, how it is impacting your own skills, and whether you are okay with that. If it bothers you, speak up. It takes more than one person to implement a counter narrative and make a change.
