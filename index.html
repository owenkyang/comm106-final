ChatGPT has become a routine part of student life with remarkable speed. Within a few years of its public release, it has been integrated into how students research, write, and study across virtually every academic discipline. The appeal is straightforward: it is fast, accessible, and produces results that feel useful. What is less visible, however, is what that convenience costs, and who benefits most from the exchange.

A recent peer-reviewed study found that 70% of students globally now use ChatGPT as part of their academic lives. That figure points to something larger than a trend in study habits. It reflects a structural shift in how knowledge work is being organized, and who controls the infrastructure that supports it. The platform frames itself as a neutral assistant, but no tool that is owned, operated, and monetized by a private corporation is truly neutral.

This essay argues that the widespread adoption of ChatGPT, particularly among students, represents a transfer of power away from individuals and toward the corporations that build and profit from these platforms. That transfer operates across three distinct dimensions: economic value, as workers become dependent on a tool that displaces rather than supplements their skills; autonomy, as corporate platforms increasingly shape how people learn and think; and privacy, as the data generated by everyday use is absorbed into systems users have little ability to see or contest.

No tool that is owned, operated, and monetized by a private corporation is truly neutral.

What the Numbers Show

The same study that found 70% of students using ChatGPT also found that most of them described it as a time-saver, a way to get through assignments faster. Adoption spans countries, disciplines, and academic levels. It is not a niche behavior anymore.
